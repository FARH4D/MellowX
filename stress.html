Dealing with Stress and Anxiety

Anxiety and stress are caused by persistent (and in some cases excessive) worry about a number of different things. If left unmanaged, stress and anxiety can start to interfere with your daily life and take a toll on your physical and mental health. Including other symptoms such as trouble sleeping, irritability or difficulty concentrating.

Although stress and anxiety are different things (stress is tied to a specific situation and is resolved when the situation is resolved, while anxiety doesn't have a specific trigger), they are still closely connected.

It's common to experience stress and anxiety from time to time, and there are strategies to help make them more manageable:

Go outside and work out

Regular exercise is one of the best ways to ease sadness and depression. This is done by:

- Releasing feel-good endorphins, natural cannabis-like brain chemicals (endogenous cannabinoids) and other natural brain chemicals that can enhance your sense of well-being.
- Taking your mind off of wories so you can get away from the cycle of negative thoughts that feed depression and anxiety.

Regular exercise also has many psychological and emotional benefits, too. It can help you:
- Gain confidence. Meeting exercise goals or challenges, even small ones, can boost your self-confidence. Getting in shape can also make you feel better about your appearance.
- Get more social interaction. Exercise and physical activity may give you the chance to meet or socialize with others. Just exchanging a friendly smile or greeting as you walk around your neighborhood can help your mood.

Meditate

While this may sound generic, meditation is an amazing way to deal with stress and anxiety because it involves deep breathing, which is a simple but efficient technique for stress reduction. It helps to clear the mind and changes your response to negative thinking.

Meditation doesn't involve getting rid of or pushing away dark thoughts, it instead helps you notice and accept them before letting them go.

Meditation can help you get to a point where you can:
- Notice the thought
- Accept it as a possibility
- Acknowledge that it is not the only possibility

Maintain healthy relationships

Friends and family are a source of great happiness in life, as well as great stress.

Studies of cortisol in hair show that children with a stable and warm family life have much lower levels of cortisol (a measure of stress) than children from homes with high levels of conflict. Within couples, conflict results in a short-term elevation in cortisol, followed by return to normal levels.

Of course, this just means that support from loved ones can also help reduce cortisol in the face of stress. For example, one study showed that having an affectionate interaction (verbally or physically) with a romantic partner or platonic friend before a stressful activity resulted in lower stress-induced markers such as heart rate and blood pressure.



Take care of a pet

Of course, relationships with animal companions can also reduce cortisol.

In one study, interaction with a therapy dog reduced distress and cortisol during a minor medical procedure in children. Another study involving 48 adults showed that contact with a dog was better than support from a friend during a socially stressful situation.

A third study tested the cortisol-reducing effect of canine companionship in pet owners compared with those who were not pet owners, and the latter group experienced a greater drop in cortisol when they were given canine companions, likely because pet owners had already benefited from the friendship of their animals at the beginning of the study

These effects are well known and are the reason many care homes and university/college campuses have introduced pet therapy as a natural cortisol and stress reducing activity.
